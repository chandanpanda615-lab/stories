## The Ten

### 1. PANCHALI KA MANN — *mythology*
**What happens:** Draupadi plucks a rare Jambu fruit meant for a fasting sage. Krishna tells the Pandavas the only way to restore it and avoid a curse is for each of them to confess their most guarded secret.

**The dilemma:** Lie and protect her reputation — condemning them all to the sage's wrath. Or tell the truth: that her heart never accepted being divided among five men — and destroy the illusion her marriage rests on.

**The lesson:** A woman's heart cannot be partitioned because duty demands it. And women are repeatedly forced into public tests of purity that ignore the private wound.

**Steal this — the Magical Truth Serum:** an external, escalating physical stake (the fruit lifting inch by inch) used to force internal confession. The pressure is visible on screen; the exposition rides on it.

> *"Maine Shiv se tapasya Karna jaise pati ko paane ke liye ki thi magar Shiv ka vardaan mere liye shraap ki tarah saabit hua..."*
> "I had done penance to Shiva to get a husband like Karna, but Shiva's boon proved to be like a curse for me..."

---

### 2. GUNAHGAAR — *relationships / moral*
**What happens:** A newly released convict travels by train to reunite with his daughter. A relentlessly chatty co-passenger, Nandini, interrogates him about his crime — then reveals she is the daughter of the man he murdered.

**The dilemma:** Nandini can take revenge — poison, or a push from the train. Or she can let him reach his own daughter, and free herself from the weight of vengeance.

**The lesson:** The prison sentence is not the punishment. Facing the wreckage of the lives you broke is. And forgiveness is finally a selfish act — it liberates the victim, it does not absolve the guilty.

**Steal this — Predator-Prey Reversal:** dramatic irony where the reader and the antagonist both know who holds the power, while the narrator thinks he is merely stuck with an annoying stranger. The trap closes slowly.

> *"I hated you. Main aapko maar daalna chahti thi train se dhakka dekar ya shayad zehar de deti aapko..."*
> "I hated you. I wanted to kill you by pushing you off the train, or maybe give you poison..."

---

### 3. CHAIN KI NEEND — *humour*
**What happens:** Ali, a carefree Bhopal tea-stall owner, finds an envelope with three lakh rupees left by a wealthy customer. The money does not fix his life — it destroys his sleep and turns him against his friends.

**The dilemma:** Keep the fortune and fund every dream, while living in permanent paranoia. Or return it to a corrupt man who never even noticed it was gone — just to sleep again.

**The lesson:** Unearned money charges an honest person a psychological tax. Poverty with a clean conscience weighs less than wealth with a stain.

**Steal this — Physicalizing Guilt:** insomnia as the narrative engine. The moral correction is driven by a bodily symptom, and it stays funny while doing it.

> *"Ek to raat bhar neend na aana phir dimaag par paison ka bojh... mast maula Ali ab baat-baat mein chirchirane laga tha."*
> "First the sleepless nights, then the burden of the money on his mind... the carefree Ali now snapped at every little thing."

### 4. ALICE — *relationships*
**What happens:** A divorced photographer struggles to connect with his nine-year-old daughter Alice on custody weekends. She insists on a fancy restaurant — and he discovers she steered them there because she knew her mother Jenny would be there with a new lover.

**The dilemma:** Play the oblivious fun-weekend dad and keep the peace. Or admit that his small daughter is quietly stage-managing the adults' collapse.

**The lesson:** Children register the fracture of a home far more sharply than adults credit — and hide that grief inside demands that look merely petulant.

**Steal this — Misdirection of Conflict:** the story presents as "father can't handle his kid." The real conflict is the child managing the parents. The surface problem is a decoy for the actual one.

> *"Maa aur main ab saath nahi hain aur hum apni marzi se alag rehna chahte hain to hamein ab life mein move on karna hoga na."*
> "Mom and I aren't together now and we chose to live apart, so we have to move on in life, don't we."

---

### 5. PYARI GUDIYA — *horror*
**What happens:** Little Binni plays with a doll her grandmother despises and finally throws away. The doll returns. The haunting escalates — and targets the grandmother's throat specifically.

**The dilemma:** Treat it as an external demon that can be discarded. Or face that it is the grandmother's own buried sin made flesh — the female foetuses she had aborted.

**The lesson:** Domestic sin and generational trauma cannot be thrown out with the rubbish. They fester, return, and go for the throat of whoever buried them.

**Steal this — The Haunted Conscience:** project a specific repressed guilt onto an inanimate object. The horror is not supernatural, it is psychological, wearing a doll.

> *"Tu soti kyun nahi, dekh kaisi raat ho gayi hai. So ja, so ja varna mandir wala shayad aa jayega."*
> "Why aren't you sleeping, look how late it is. Sleep, sleep, or the temple one might come."

---

### 6. ASAVARI — *motivational*
**What happens:** Honest SDM Asavari halts a powerful builder's illegal mall construction to save a park. The builder tries to bribe her — using her own boyfriend Karthik as the courier.

**The dilemma:** Look away and keep the man she loves. Or prosecute, knowing Karthik goes down with the builder.

**The lesson:** Integrity is not one heroic act. It is a grinding, isolating swim against the current that keeps charging you your closest relationships.

**Steal this — The Compromised Ally:** don't put duty against a stranger. Put duty against the person the protagonist loves, by making the lover the antagonist's instrument.

> *"Yeh paise builder ne mere hathon bhijwaye hain... wo mera dost I mean janta hai mujhe."*
> "The builder sent this money through my hands... he's my friend, I mean, he knows me."

---

### 7. NAYE SAAL KA TOHFA — *relationships* ⚠️ *authorship uncredited*
**What happens:** Munmun misses her Badi Amma, who her parents sent to the village claiming she "wanted to go." On a school trip to an old age home to distribute gifts, Munmun finds her there.

**The dilemma:** The parents must hold the lie and lose their daughter's respect permanently — or admit their cruelty and bring the grandmother home.

**The lesson:** Children learn morality from how their parents treat the weakest person in the family, not from what their parents preach.

**Steal this — The Innocent Lens:** a naive narrator who doesn't grasp the adults' malice exposes hypocrisy without a word of sermon.

> *"Teacher to bol rahi thi ki wo log bahut bure hote hain jo apne mummy papa ko old age home mein rakhte hain, to kya uske mummy papa bhi..."*
> "Teacher was saying people who put their parents in an old age home are very bad — so are her mummy-papa also..."

---

### 8. KHWAAB PAHELI — *relationships* ⚠️ *title credits Neelesh Misra, not her — verify before studying*
**What happens:** Teenager Aashi has recurring nightmares of drowning in the Narmada, slipping from her mother's hands. Her mother Meera hunts for a cure and finds the dream is rooted in Aashi's hidden guilt over her grandfather's death.

**The dilemma:** Aashi is caught between mourning Baba and a secret conviction that she willed his death — because he had confided to her that he was in unbearable pain and wanted to go.

**The lesson:** Without a safe place to process death, a child's grief mutates into self-blame. Compassion curdles into haunting.

**Steal this — The Nightmare as Cipher:** the recurring dream is not atmosphere, it is a puzzle the characters must decode. Solving it *is* the healing.

> *"Maa, Baba jeena nahi chahte the, wo marna chahte the. Maa, unhone bola mujhko."*
> "Mom, Baba didn't want to live, he wanted to die. Mom, he told me."

---

### 9. LAMBA SAFAR — *relationships*
**What happens:** Vini finds a hidden photograph revealing she is not adopted but the biological, illegitimate daughter of her mother Akhila. Betrayed, she runs to Kerala to find the estranged grandfather — forcing Akhila back to the past she fled twenty years ago.

**The dilemma:** Akhila can keep the past locked and protect the independent life she built. Or return to the father she shamed and the home she abandoned, to give her daughter an identity.

**The lesson:** We lie to the people we love to spare them our pain — and the lie and the distance turn out to be the thing that wounds them.

**Steal this — Parallel Abandonment:** the daughter's running away in the present mirrors the mother's running away in the past, and forces its resolution. One structural rhyme does the work of ten pages of backstory.

> *"Naana main Vini... aapki beti, I mean aapki Akhila ki beti."*
> "Grandpa, I'm Vini... your daughter — I mean, your Akhila's daughter."

### 10. NANI KI CHAKORI AUR CHAKORI KI NANI — *children's* ⚠️ *weakest source; quote does not match plot*
**What happens:** Chakori has no ribbons or coloured paper for a school hat competition, and Nani's sewing box is empty. The birds they feed every day drop bright feathers onto her plain paper cap.

**The dilemma:** Surrender to what they don't have — or find beauty in what is already around them.

**The lesson:** Kindness sent outward, even something as small as feeding birds, builds a circle of care that returns when you are most exposed.

**Steal this — Nature as a Character:** the environment is not backdrop, it is an active participant that answers a character's kindness.
