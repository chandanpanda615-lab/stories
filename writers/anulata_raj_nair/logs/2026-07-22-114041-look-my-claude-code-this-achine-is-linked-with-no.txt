     Step 3 — The query battery (the actual substance)

     Run against notebook_query, reusing conversation_id across calls so context
     carries. Group into 3 calls, not 9 — each query is slow. Every query ends
     with the same evidence clause:

     ▎ "Quote her actual lines as evidence, written in roman-script Hinglish

     ▎ find a real line in the sources, say so — do not invent or paraphrase a 
     ▎ quote as if it were verbatim."

     Query A — entry & architecture
     1. Opening move — how the first lines land. Does she open on an object, a
     line of dialogue, a domestic action, a wound already in progress? What
     contract does the opening make with the listener?
     2. Relationship architecture — who wants what from whom; the power
     imbalance; the unspoken debt or obligation holding people together.
     3. Texture — the concrete domestic detail (kitchen, chai, dupatta, a phone
     that doesn't ring) she uses to carry emotion instead of stating it.

     Query B — the pressure system
     4. The pause / thehrāv — where she slows, stops, lets silence do the work.
     What she deliberately withholds and for how long.
     5. The dilemma — the moral fork with no clean answer. How she loads both
     sides so neither choice is free.
     6. Subtext — lines where what is said is not what is meant. The sentence
     that means its opposite.

     Query C — the turn & the landing
     7. Perspective turn — the moment the character we were judging gets their
     reason. Where in the story it lands, and how she sets it up so it reverses
     the listener instead of excusing the character.
     8. Ending — open vs resolved, and what the last line is doing (a question?
     an image? a small ordinary act?).
     9. Narrator's voice — sentence rhythm, distance from the character, direct
     address to the listener, where she speaks as herself.

     For Apply and Seeds modes: still run A–C first (grounded in her real
     craft), then apply the extracted patterns to the user's draft/theme. Never
     critique or generate from generic "emotional Hindi story" instincts — every
     recommendation must trace to something the notebook actually showed.

● craft\2026-07-22-lamba-safar.md

  What this story is actually doing

  The title is the structure. Lamba Safar names three journeys stacked on one
  phrase: Vini's bus to Kottayam, Akhila's eighteen-year fall backwards into
  memory ("Akhila to jaise kisi lambi yatra par nikal gayi thi. Atharah baras 
  lambi yatra"), and the twenty-year distance to her father's doorstep ("ek bada
  lamba safar tay kiya tha, thak gayi thi"). Same words, three magnitudes. The
  title isn't a label on the story — it's the spine of it.

  The best three lines in the piece are easy to miss. The narration establishes
  an image: the daughter's voice dissolving into her mother's "jaise paani mein 
  shakkar" — like sugar in water. Vini then reaches for what that implies: "Meri
  aawaz bilkul aap ke jaisi hai na maa" — my voice is just like yours, isn't
  it. And Akhila deflects using the same metaphor to take it back: "To mishri ki
  dali hai wo meethi" — it's the rock sugar that's sweet.

  On the surface, a mother being modest about her singing. Underneath, Vini is
  saying I am biologically yours, admit it, and Akhila is answering the 
  sweetness belongs to an object, not to blood. She kills the genetic claim
  inside the exact image the narration just built. Neither of them says the real
  word. That's the whole craft of the story in three lines.

  The misdirection is honest. When Vini vanishes, we're locked inside Akhila's
  panic — has she gone looking for Bhushan? — so we spend the entire middle
  braced for the daughter to walk into the house of the man who abandoned her
  mother. She walks into her grandfather's house instead. We were never lied to
  by the narrator. We inherited a mother's fear and drew the wrong conclusion
  ourselves.

  And the inversion that makes Akhila un-condemnable: she didn't flee a cruel
  father. She fled a loving one — a man who made her rasam rice when she felt
  sick in early pregnancy. She ran because she loved him too much to hand him
  her shame. Once you know that, twenty years of silence stops reading as
  cowardice.

  The ending refuses a speech. Akhila sits on the threshold and says nothing;
  Nana looks at her, smiles, and goes to make coffee — for both of them, which
  admits the granddaughter in the same gesture. She crossed the country and
  couldn't cross the last step. He crosses it for her.

  One honest craft note

  She states her thesis out loud near the end — "hum kai baar hamare apno se 
  itna pyaar karte hain ki unko takleef na ho isliye unse man nahi karte, yeh 
  galat hai." By prose-fiction standards that's a flaw; it distrusts the images
  already built. But this is written to be heard once, with no page to turn back
  to. What saves it: she states the moral and then refuses to end on it. The
  last thing that happens is the coffee. She tells you the meaning, then hands
  you an image that proves it, and lets the image be last.
